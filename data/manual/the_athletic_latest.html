Women’s NCAA Tournament Bracket Watch: UConn, UCLA No. 1 seed locks. Then who?

Regardless of Big Ten tournament performance, UCLA has done enough for an NCAA Tournament No. 1 seed. Brian Rothmuller / Icon Sportswire via Getty Images)

By Eden Laase
March 6, 2026Updated March 7, 2026

Editor’s note: This article is part of the Bracket Central series, an inside look at the run-up to the men’s & women’s NCAA Tournaments, along with analysis and picks during the tournaments.

Things change quickly in bracketology. The chaos increases exponentially during conference tournaments, when games are happening constantly and every team is desperate to make the most out of its final opportunities.

What’s true today could be different tomorrow. That’s especially true for teams on the bubble and teams on the line between No. 4 and No. 5. The top 16 teams host the first two rounds, which provides a huge advantage. Falling to a No. 5 seed can be devastating, while rising to a No. 4 seed is cause for celebration.

Here’s where everyone stands … subject to change as soon as the next game tips, of course. Embrace the madness!

Could the No. 1 seeds change?

Anything can happen in March! Well, almost anything. I don’t see UConn losing in the Big East tournament, and even if that happened, the Huskies have done enough to keep their No. 1 seed. The same goes for UCLA. The Bruins have arguably the best resume in the country, and after collecting 14 Quad 1 wins, they also have a top seed locked up.

South Carolina and Texas also have excellent resumes, but they are more vulnerable than the other two No. 1 seeds, simply because LSU and Vanderbilt are close behind. If the Tigers or the Commodores were to win the SEC tournament, then they would jump into the No. 1 seed conversation. Vandy and Texas have flip-flopped a couple of times this season, which means Texas would be the more likely squad to lose its No. 1 seed.

Are Duke and Louisville affected by recent upsets?

Nothing is straightforward in bracketology, so the answer is a resounding “kind of.” Both are currently safe on the No. 3 line, even after Duke lost to Clemson on Feb. 22 and Louisville fell to Virginia on the same day and to Notre Dame on March 1. But if Louisville loses to Syracuse on Friday, that combination of recent losses would likely be enough to drop the Cardinals to a No. 4 seed.

Duke is the top No. 3 seed, so the Blue Devils are a bit safer. But if they lose to Clemson (again), that could open the door for Maryland, Kentucky, Ohio State or Minnesota to rise, depending on their conference tournament results.

Is Michigan State out of the hosting conversation?

Losing to Illinois to start the Big Ten tournament was the latest in a tough sequence for Michigan State. Recency matters to the committee, and despite having a solid overall resume, the Spartans have struggled down the stretch. Michigan State has lost six of its last nine games, and with the other No. 4 and No. 5 seeds still playing in their conference tournaments, Michigan State is the only team of the bunch that can no longer improve its credentials. I don’t see the Spartans falling any further, but hosting seems to be out of the equation.

Maryland also suffered a second-round loss to Oregon, which moves the Terrapins from the top No. 4 seed to the last No. 4 seed. Maryland finished its regular season strong, winning six of its last seven, which included victories over Michigan State and Ohio State. That keeps the Terrapins as a host team for now.

Can another team jump to a No. 4 seed?

Maryland is obviously vulnerable after its loss, but the Terrapins aren’t the only team that could fall. With Minnesota and Ohio State facing off Friday in the Big Ten tournament, the loser will be at risk. Beating Arizona State, the No. 54 team in the NET rankings, wouldn’t be enough for West Virginia to make the jump, but if the Mountaineers beat the Sun Devils and then top either Baylor or Colorado in the semifinals, they would put themselves in discussion for a No. 4 seed. North Carolina is in a similar situation, especially thanks to its win over Duke on Sunday.

If UNC advances to the ACC championship game, then a No. 4 seed becomes attainable. Winning the ACC tournament — a possibility for the Tar Heels — would ensure a hosting seed.
